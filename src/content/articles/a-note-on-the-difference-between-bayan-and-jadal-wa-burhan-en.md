## A Note on the Difference Between Bayan and Jadal wa Burhan

This article reflects on a useful distinction drawn in a commentary on Imam al-Shafi'i's *al-Risalah*: the difference between *bayan* on the one hand and *jadal* and *burhan* on the other. At times, clear explanation seizes the mind and heart so strongly that a person feels compelled toward acceptance and submission.

The article explains that in Arabic, *bayan* conveys clarity, exposition, and the making of meaning plain before the listener. It can also refer to decisive proof and eloquent speech. In this sense, what logicians sometimes call *burhan* overlaps with what Arabic usage may call *bayan*. The hadith, "Indeed, some eloquence is like magic," is cited to show how powerful speech can influence the soul and make even a weak idea seem persuasive.

Yet the article warns that there is a difference between sound explanatory clarity and the culture of argument for its own sake. Classical teachers of logic often trained students to prove a claim and then dismantle it from the opposite side in order to sharpen disputation skills. This produced what the article calls a method of *jadal*, a style of contentious debate that some revealed texts warn against.

Qur'anic and Prophetic warnings about excessive disputation are then invoked, along with the virtue of leaving fruitless argument even when one is right. The conclusion is that ongoing wrangling and polemical quarrelling rarely produce good results. Rather, they often sever affection and deepen division.
